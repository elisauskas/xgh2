About XGH 2nd Edition

The XGH (eXtreme Go Horse) methodology was originally conceptualized by Brazilian developers and documented in the legendary 22 Axioms of the Extreme Go Horse Methodology. It revolutionized software development by acknowledging what every developer knew but few dared to say: sometimes you just need to get things done, regardless of best practices.

This second edition builds upon those foundational principles while incorporating the realities of modern software development. We now live in an era of cloud-native applications, microservices architectures, AI-assisted coding, and most importantly, vibe-driven development.

The original XGH philosophy emerged from the trenches of real-world software development, where developers learned that pragmatism often trumps perfection. This updated edition expands from 22 to 23 axioms, adding modern context for the post-AI era while maintaining the irreverent spirit that made XGH a cult classic among developers worldwide.

Whether you're dealing with Kubernetes clusters that have achieved sentience, microservices that communicate through passive-aggressive error messages, or stakeholders who change requirements faster than you can implement them, these axioms will guide you through the chaos with humor and hard-won wisdom.

The 23 Axioms

Axiom 1
If it compiles and the tests pass, it's basically production-ready. If it doesn't compile but the PM is breathing down your neck, comment out the broken parts and ship it anyway. The users will debug it for free.
Axiom 2
Vibe-driven development trumps test-driven development. If the code feels right, it probably is.
Axiom 3
ChatGPT is your senior architect. Prompt with confidence, ship with courage, maintain with delegation.
Axiom 4
Documentation is what AI generates when you paste your code and ask "please document this mess."
Axiom 5
Technical debt is just future job security. Thank your past self for the guaranteed employment.
Axiom 6
If your system architecture can't be explained using only farm animal noises and interpretive dance, you've overengineered it. "Moo-moo-oink-CRASH" should adequately describe your microservices mesh.
Axiom 7
The best time to refactor was six months ago. The second best time is when you're about to quit. The third best time is when your replacement starts.
Axiom 8
Premature optimization is the root of all evil. Premature planning is the root of all delays.
Axiom 9
A panicked commit at 2 AM with the message "IT'S WORKING DON'T TOUCH ANYTHING" is worth a thousand peer reviews. If you can't explain why it works, nobody can explain why it broke.
Axiom 10
If the demo works, the feature is done. Everything else is just polish for the perfectionists.
Axiom 11
Friday deployments build character. Character builds legends. Legends build startups.
Axiom 12
Your local environment is the source of truth. If it works on your machine, the cloud is wrong.
Axiom 13
Microservices are just a way to turn one problem into 47 distributed problems that all blame each other. It's like a dysfunctional family reunion, but with more JSON and fewer awkward hugs.
Axiom 14
Staging environments are where code goes to pretend it works before failing spectacularly in production. Skip the middleman‚Äîproduction IS your staging environment.
Axiom 15
A good developer codes with their gut and Claude. A great developer codes with their gut, Claude, and a junior developer to blame.
Axiom 16
Stand-ups are theater for management. The real work happens in bathroom conversations, elevator debugging sessions, and that one Slack DM where someone admits they don't know what their microservice actually does.
Axiom 17
Modern pair programming is you, three AI chatbots, and Stack Overflow having a four-way argument about semicolons while your rubber duck judges silently from the corner.
Axiom 18
If you're not breaking things, you're a junior developer. If you're breaking everything, you're a senior developer having a bad day. If you're breaking things selectively and blaming AWS, you're ready for promotion.
Axiom 19
In code reviews, the person asking "What does this function do?" has never been oncall. The person who approves with just "LGTM üëç" has seen things that would make a kernel developer weep.
Axiom 20
Every problem can be solved by adding another layer of abstraction, middleware, or microservice. If that doesn't work, add a message queue. If that still doesn't work, blame it on eventual consistency and go home.
Axiom 21
Design patterns are suggestions for people who can't vibe their way to a solution. Trust the flow, embrace the spaghetti.
Axiom 22
Kubernetes is what happens when 47 Google engineers get together and ask "How can we make deploying a simple web app feel like launching a space shuttle?" The answer involves 12 YAML files, 3 existential crises, and a sacrifice to the networking gods.
Axiom 23
The code that survives is not the most elegant or efficient, but the code that nobody understands well enough to delete. Write your functions like cryptic incantations‚Äîfuture developers will be too scared to touch them.
